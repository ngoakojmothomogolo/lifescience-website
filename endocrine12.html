Endocrine System and Homeostasis

1. What is the Endocrine System?

The endocrine system consists of glands that produce and secrete hormones directly into the bloodstream.

Hormones are chemical messengers that travel to target organs where they bring about a response.

2. Characteristics of Hormones

  • Secreted by endocrine glands
  • Transported by the bloodstream
  • Act on specific target organs
  • Slow acting but long-lasting effects

3. Difference Between Endocrine and Nervous System

Endocrine System Nervous System
Uses hormones Uses electrical impulses
Slow response Fast response
Long-lasting effects Short-lived effects
Transported by blood Transported by neurons

4. Major Endocrine Glands

  • Pituitary gland (master gland)
  • Hypothalamus
  • Thyroid gland
  • Adrenal glands
  • Pancreas
  • Gonads (testes and ovaries)

5. Homeostasis

Homeostasis is the maintenance of a constant internal environment in the body.

This includes regulation of:

  • Body temperature
  • Blood glucose levels
  • Water balance

6. Regulation of Blood Glucose Levels (VERY IMPORTANT)

6.1 When Blood Glucose is HIGH

Process:

  1. Pancreas detects high blood glucose levels
  2. Pancreas secretes insulin
  3. Insulin is transported in the blood to target cells
  4. Cells take up glucose
  5. Excess glucose is converted into glycogen in the liver and muscles
  6. Blood glucose level decreases to normal

6.2 When Blood Glucose is LOW

Process:

  1. Pancreas detects low blood glucose levels
  2. Pancreas secretes glucagon
  3. Glucagon is transported to the liver
  4. Glycogen is converted into glucose
  5. Glucose is released into the bloodstream
  6. Blood glucose level increases to normal

7. Negative Feedback Mechanism

Negative feedback is a process where the body reverses a change to maintain a stable internal environment.

Example: Blood glucose regulation

  • Increase in glucose → insulin released → glucose decreases
  • Decrease in glucose → glucagon released → glucose increases

8. Regulation of Body Temperature (Thermoregulation)

8.1 When Body Temperature is High

  1. Hypothalamus detects high temperature
  2. Blood vessels in the skin dilate (vasodilation)
  3. Sweat glands produce sweat
  4. Heat is lost through evaporation
  5. Body temperature decreases

8.2 When Body Temperature is Low

  1. Hypothalamus detects low temperature
  2. Blood vessels constrict (vasoconstriction)
  3. Less heat is lost
  4. Muscles contract (shivering)
  5. Heat is produced
  6. Body temperature increases

9. Role of the Pituitary Gland

The pituitary gland is known as the “master gland” because it controls other endocrine glands.

It is controlled by the hypothalamus.

10. Hormonal Control (Hypothalamus and Pituitary)

The hypothalamus detects changes in the internal environment and sends signals to the pituitary gland.

The pituitary gland then secretes hormones that regulate other glands.

11. Effects of Hormonal Imbalance

  • Diabetes mellitus: Insufficient insulin → high blood glucose
  • Goitre: Iodine deficiency affects thyroid function
  • Dwarfism: Lack of growth hormone
  • Gigantism: Excess growth hormone

12. Summary (Exam Ready)

  • Endocrine system uses hormones for communication
  • Hormones are slow but long-lasting
  • Homeostasis maintains a stable internal environment
  • Insulin lowers blood glucose
  • Glucagon raises blood glucose
  • Negative feedback maintains balance
  • Thermoregulation controls body temperature
