Evolution

1. What is Evolution?

Evolution is the change in the characteristics of a population over time.

It occurs through changes in allele frequencies in a population.

2. Evidence for Evolution

  • Fossil record: Shows changes in organisms over time
  • Comparative anatomy: Similar structures indicate common ancestry
  • Embryology: Similar stages of development in embryos
  • Genetics: Similar DNA and proteins suggest common ancestry
  • Biogeography: Distribution of species across the Earth

3. Darwin’s Theory of Natural Selection

Key points:

  • There is variation in a population
  • Organisms produce more offspring than can survive
  • There is a struggle for survival
  • Individuals with favourable traits survive and reproduce
  • These traits are passed on to offspring

This results in survival of the fittest.

4. Factors that Lead to Evolution

  • Mutation
  • Genetic variation
  • Natural selection
  • Genetic drift
  • Migration (gene flow)

5. Types of Selection

5.1 Directional Selection

Favour one extreme phenotype.

5.2 Stabilising Selection

Favour intermediate phenotype and eliminate extremes.

5.3 Disruptive Selection

Favour both extreme phenotypes and eliminate intermediate.

6. Speciation

Speciation is the formation of new species from existing species.

Types:

  • Allopatric speciation: Occurs due to geographical isolation
  • Sympatric speciation: Occurs without geographical separation

7. Reproductive Isolation

Reproductive isolation prevents interbreeding between populations.

Types:

  • Geographical isolation
  • Ecological isolation
  • Temporal isolation
  • Behavioural isolation

8. Evidence for Evolution (Detailed)

8.1 Fossils

Preserved remains or traces of organisms from the past.

8.2 Homologous Structures

Structures with the same origin but different functions (e.g. human arm and bat wing).

8.3 Analogous Structures

Structures with different origins but same function (e.g. wings of birds and insects).

9. Human Evolution

Humans evolved from ape-like ancestors over millions of years.

  • Increased brain size
  • Bipedalism (walking on two legs)
  • Tool usage

10. Controversy: Lamarck vs Darwin

Lamarck

  • Use and disuse
  • Acquired characteristics are inherited

Darwin

  • Natural selection
  • Only genetic traits are inherited

11. Modern Synthesis

Combines Darwin’s theory with genetics to explain evolution.

  • Evolution = change in allele frequencies
  • Mutation provides variation
  • Natural selection acts on variation

12. Natural Selection Process

  • There is a great deal of variation amongst the offspring.
  • Some have favourable characteristics and some do not.
  • When there is a change in the environmental conditions or if there is competition, then organisms with characteristics, which make them more suited, survive whilst organisms with unfavourable characteristics, which make them less suited, die.
  • The organisms that survive, reproduce and thus, pass on the allele for the favourable characteristic to their offspring.
  • The next generation will therefore have a higher proportion of individuals with the favourable characteristic.
