Life Science Paper 2 Biological Terms

DNA and RNA

  • DNA Profiling: the analysis of DNA samples to identify individuals that may be related.
  • Nucleotide: a monomer of nucleic acids.
  • tRNA: type of RNA containing anticodons.
  • DNA Replication: the process whereby DNA makes an exact copy of itself.
  • Anticodon: the triplet bases found on tRNA molecule.
  • Transcription: the process whereby mRNA is formed from DNA molecule.
  • Translation: the process of arranging amino acids according to the sequence of bases on the mRNA.
  • Anticodon: the triplet of bases found on a tRNA molecule.
  • Ribosome: cell organelle to which mRNA attaches to during protein synthesis.
  • Ribosome: cell organelle where translation occur.
  • Codons: base triplets found on mRNA.
  • Deoxyribose sugar: the type of sugar found in DNA.
  • Chromatin: tangled network of DNA and protein located within the nuclues.
  • Double-Helix: the natural shape/structure of a DNA molecule.
  • Hydrogen bond: the bonds that hold the two strands of a DNA molecule together.
  • Peptide bonds: bonds that join amino acids together.
  • Gene: sections of DNA that carry hereditary information.

Meiosis

  • Interphase: the phase of the cell cycle where the cell grows and prepare for division.
  • Metaphase I: the phase of meiosis where homologous chromosomes align at the equator of the cell.
  • Crossing Over: the exchange of genetic material between homologous chromosomes during cell division.
  • Homologous Chromosomes: chromosomes that carry the same genes but may have different alleles.
  • Karyokinesis: the division of nucleus during cell division.
  • Centromere: the region of a chromosome where the two sister chromatids are attached.
  • Autosomes: non-sex chromosomes that determine traits not directly related to sex.
  • Down Syndrome: the genetic disorder caused by an extra copy of chromosome 21.
  • Chiasma: the point where two homologous chromosomes cross over during meiosis.
  • Non-Disjunction: the failure of chromosome pairs to separate properly during meiosis.
  • Centrioles: organelles that form the spindle fibers during cell division.
  • Cytokinesis: the division of the cytoplasm during cell division.
  • Ovary: the site of meiosis in females.
  • Testes: the site of meiosis in males.

Genetics and Inheritance

  • Locus: the position of a gene on a chromosome.
  • Homozygous: organisms having two identical alles at a given locus.
  • Heterozygous: organism having two non-identical alleles at a given locus.
  • Phenotype: the physical and functional expression of a gene.
  • Monohybrid: a genetic cross involving one and its alleles.
  • Alleles: two or more alternative forms of a gene at the same locus.
  • Gene: a segment of a chromosome that codes for a particular characteristic.
  • Biotechnology: the use of living organisms and their biological processes to improve the quality of human life.
  • Recessive: an allele that is expressed phenotypically only in the homozygous condition.
  • Dominant: an allele that is always expressed in the phenotype.
  • Gene: a segment of a chromosome that codes for a particular characteristic.
  • Haemophilia: an inherited disorder where blood fails to clot properly.
  • Co-Dominance: the type of inheritance involving alleles that are equally determine the phenotype of heterozygous offspring.
  • Incomplete Dominance: the type of inheritance involving two alleles that are not dominant over one another.
  • Sex-linked: characteristics controlled by genes which are located on the sex chromosomes.
  • Pedigree Diagram: a diagram showing the inheritance of genetic disorders over many generations.
  • Karyotype: the number, shape and arrangement of all chromosomes in the nucleus of somatic cell.
  • Genome: all the genes that make up an organism.
  • Gentics: the study of hereditary and variation in organisms.
  • Genetic engineering: the manipulation of genetic material of an organism to get desired changes.
  • Cloning: the production of a genetically identical copy of an organism using biotechnology.

Evolution

  • Theory: an explanation for something that has been observed in nature and which can be supported by facts, laws and tested hypothesis.
  • Biogeography: present day distribution of living organisms.
  • Species: organisms with similar characteristics that are able to interbreed randomly and produce fertile offspring.
  • Fossils: remains of life forms preserved in rocks, ice, and dried up sap trees.
  • Artificial Selection: the selection of desirable characteristics by humans.
  • Continuous variation: a type of variation where there is a range of phenotype for the same characteristics.
  • Discontinuos Variation: a type of variation in a population with no intermediate phenotype.
  • Homologous Structures: structures in different organisms that have similar basic plan which suggest that they share common ancestor.
  • Natural Selection: the process that enables organisms with desirable characteristics to survive and reproduce in a particular environment.
  • Punctuated Equilibrium: an explanation describing evolution as consisting a long phases and little change alternating with short phases of rapid change.
  • Stereoscopic/Binocular Vision: the type of vision shared by apes and humans that allows for depth perception.
  • Biological Evolution: change in the characteristics of species over time.
  • Bipedalism: an upright posture and walking only on two legs.
  • Canine: large, pointed teeth in African apes that are used for tearing food.
  • Cranium: the part of the skull that houses/protects the brain.
  • Extinction: the permanent disapppearance of species from earth.
  • Foramen magnum: the opening of the skull through which spinal cord passes.
  • Hominidae: family to which humans belong.
  • Phylogenetic tree: a diagrammatic representation showing possible evolutionary relationships between different species.
  • Prognathous: having a protuding jaw.
  • Quadrapedal: the act of walking on all four limbs.
  • Speciation: the process whereby now species are formed.
